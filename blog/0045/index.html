10 Person Analogy
By Junho (April 07, 2024) back

While discussing Bitcoin with a friend who is a long-timeframe skeptic, he proposed an argument against Bitcoin I've heard many times before, but framed it with an interesting analogy. This post is my response to that argument.

The Oft-Heard Argument Against Bitcoin

This common argument against Bitcoin is that most of the supply is already scooped up by a tiny fraction of people. Thus it can never succeed in achieving full adoption because it exacerbates or simply redistributes extreme wealth inequality, and the majority of people, the laggards who didn't accumulate Bitcoin "early", will refuse to participate in such a system.

Reframing it - 10 person village analogy

However, the analogy my friend presented was a good one. It went like this - imagine you have a village with 10 people.

One day, one of the people decides to create a new form of money, and acquires an extremely large amount of the supply.

Perhaps one, perhaps two other people decide the money is promising and acquire some themselves.

But once the supply distribution is overly skewed to 1-3 people, the remaining 7-9 people have no incentive to trade their scarce economic goods for the newly created money and buy into the system. Instead, they will keep their economic goods or demand some other form of good as payment.

In fact, the 7-9 people might decide to create their own form of money, fairly distributed among them somehow, and use that instead.

Surely Bitcoin will face this same situation - once it becomes large enough, people or organizations which are "late" and unable to acquire significant amounts of Bitcoin will simply create their own version of Bitcoin, or refuse to accept it, since by accepting it, they would be willing to trade all their scarce economic goods for a money which has already all been cheaply accumulated by early adopters.

Response 1 - Stock to flow

My immediate reaction is to quote Saifedean. Money is not some "shared illusion", it's a practical technology which competes on economic realities. Specifically, stock to flow ratio.

If the economy is divided into two factions, and one accepts Bitcoin and the other accepts some economic good with a lower stock to flow ratio, then all else being equal, the economic faction that accepts the lower stock to flow good will be vulnerable to economic attack.

Going back to the 10 person analogy, if the laggard majority economic faction accepts some debasing fiat currency or low stock to flow good as money, then that faction could be subject to economic attack. Their economic energy could get sucked out at the rate at which the Bitcoin economic faction outproduces that debasing fiat currency or low stock to flow economic good.

If the Bitcoin clone of the laggard economic majority faction has the same credible scarcity as Bitcoin, then this argument I'm making doesn't apply.

Response 2 - Prisoner's Dilemna

In the scale of states, the losers of the fiat monetary system (everyone other than the world reserve currency issuer) have the most incentive to be early Bitcoin adopters.

It's the same way that within a state, the losers of the fiat monetary system (average people) have the incentive to adopt a scarce money, through which they can no longer be ravaged by the winners of the fiat monetary system, which is the cantillionaire mafia.

While the state Bitcoin competition is only on it's first step (only El Salvador adopting Bitcoin), the competition within states is much more developed.

In the case of the US, with significant ETF inflows, within a few months of trading, we can see that there are almost certainly cantillionaire mafia insiders who decide to buy some Bitcoin. There will likely be more.

Essentially, there is an incentive for individuals in the cantillionaire mafia to hedge, or simply join the Bitcoin peasants.

States are made of people as well, not just the cantillionaire mafia, and I think the same dynamic will hold true for state-level adoption of Bitcoin.

Conclusion

While I don't think Bitcoin can be stopped by a laggard economic faction, I do agree that laggard economic factions will find it hard to accept that they failed to accumulate significant portions of the world's next money, and it's possible that such factions will attempt to ban or clone Bitcoin.

While I don't think any such attempt will stop Bitcoin, and I also don't think any such Bitcoin clone could ever replace Bitcoin, due to Bitcoin's established history and network effect as decentralized scarce money, I do think that Bitcoin may have to overcome significant hurdles to reach 100% adoption.
