The Declining American Empire
By Junho (July 21, 2023) back

The American Empire is declining. Here's a wonderful video by Ray Dalio which is a good introduction to this fact.

The US has limited and finite resources (all resources are limited) and is deterministically spending them down. The 3 broad categories of those resources, from which all of America's power stems, are:

  • media
  • money
  • military

If the current trajectory continues, the US will continue to spend down its resources, lose power, and may eventually collapse.

Resource 1: Media

I use "media" as a catch-all term to encompass most forms of soft power.

The US's global reputation has gone from that of a prosperous, strong, model nation to that of a declining, bankrupt nation that loves to print money, meddle in foreign countries' domestic affairs, and wage wars all over the world. Couple that with all kinds of problems such as cultural issues, excessive drug use, mass shootings, political divide, and wealth gaps, and the US is no longer a nation that other nations want to be like.

By losing its reputation (soft power), the US has lost access to all kinds of influence and benefits that it used to enjoy when it had more soft power.

Domestically, many of its own subjects no longer trust the media outlets of the establishment. This includes television programs such as CNBC and newspapers such as The New York Times. However, this applies not just to media outlets but to pretty much the entire US government. For example, many people don't trust US government:

  • figureheads such as Fauci and Yellen
  • presidents such as Biden, Obama, and Bush
  • agencies such as the CIA and SEC

The list goes on and on. Once its subjects lose trust in it, the US government isn't able to control or guide the behavior of its subjects to its benefit, and becomes weaker.

US citizens didn't always distrust the US government. But the US used up and lost the trust of its subjects by being dishonest and repeatedly not acting in their best interest. Trust, at the end of the day, is a finite resource.

Resource 2: Money

I use "money" to refer to the various aspects of the financial health of the US.

The US hasn't run a trade surplus since 1975. This means as a country, it consumes more from other countries than it produces for other countries.

The US government hasn't run a budget surplus since 2001:

US Budget Deficit

In FY 2022, the US federal deficit was 1.38 trillion!

This might all be fine if the US had a large glut of savings it could spend down in order to finance these deficits, but the US government is financing its spending by borrowing money:

US Federal Debt

Borrowing money isn't anything new but we have to look at the scale here:

  • First of all, the US has way too much debt. It's debt to GDP ratio is 130%, and it's debt to revenue is 6:1. The US hasn't run a budget surplus since 2001 which means it hasn't had a penny of profit for 20 years! If you're financing your spending using debt, then the only way to pay down debt is by running a budget surplus.
  • Second of all, the US has actually been forced to print money (which it marks on its balance sheet as "debt" using accounting sleight-of-hand by printing and then lending to itself) because there isn't enough money that people and countries around the world are willing to lend to the US. Here's the balance sheet of the Federal Reserve, which is basically a fancy word for the US money printer:

Federal Reserve Balance Sheet

The US has been able to print so much money without causing hyperinflation because the dollar is used and held in reserves for trade all over the world. People in countries with weaker currencies (such as Argentina) also save in the dollar. This allows the US to export its inflation overseas, thus stealing not only from US citizens but from the entire world population.

However, de-dollarization (ceasing to use dollars for trade) has been picking up steam recently after the US froze Russia's forex reserves. The US also weaponized the dollar system when it stole money from Aghanistan. Many countries, especially in Asia, the Middle East, Africa, and South America have been starting to trade in local currencies.

De-dollarization also means that the US will have trouble financing its extreme deficit spending by borrowing. Typically, those who save in dollars end up buying Treasuries (ie lending to the US government) in order to earn what is considered a risk-free yield. So naturally if there is less demand to hold and use dollars, the US will have a lower capacity to borrow money, forcing the US to either spend less or finance its spending by print money. Historically the US has chosen to print money (observe Fed balance sheet above).

De-dollarization also leads to inflation because when countries stop using dollars, the dollars don't just get destroyed. The dollars are sold or spent back into the Western economy which leads to inflation because there is a greater supply of dollars.

The US is also clamping down surveillance and limitations on the dollar domestically. The more domestic control there is on dollar usage, the more the dollar goes from being money to being government sponsored social credits or vouchers or something of that nature. Recently the Fed launched FedNow which is basically a CBDC, and increases control on domestic dollar usage.

Such domestic controls devalue the dollar. To understand this, think about whether these two amounts of money are worth the same:

  • $100 that you are free to use any way you like
  • $100 that you can only spend on government-approved goods and services

I think most would agree that the money in the first option is worth more. Such devaluation doesn't show up in the dollar denomination but results in decreased demand to use and hold dollars, which eventually shows up as a decrease in purchasing power via inflation.

Finally, the US economy is pretty hollowed out; a lot of manufacturing has moved overseas where the cost of labor is cheaper. Increasingly, what's left of the US economy is voodoo financial services and accounting magic, usually propped up by dollar printing. This part of the US economy will become worth less and less as the world moves to hard money such as gold or Bitcoin.

There are also various cultural forces which weaken the US economy by making it inefficient and unproductive but I'll save that discussion for another time.

Resource 3: Military

The US has a massive military; it spends more on military spending than the next 10 countries combined.

The US can print as much money as it wants. But once the dollar is devalued enough through too much dollar printing and de-dollarization, the US won't be able to maintain such a massive military because a military costs money.

Once the US can't finance its military anymore, that's when we get into the dangerous situation of "use it now or lose it" in terms of military power.

The US also has some last resort options such as martial law and a draft that can provide some final boosts to its military power. Hopefully we never get to a situation where these are used offensively in a last resort bid for power.

The US also has WMD such as nukes but hopefully the people in power aren't so desperate and evil that they would destroy the world rather than let the US quietly fade away.

Conclusion

Everything can be viewed of in terms of ROI. In order for the US to gain power, it has to invest resources in endeavours that have a positive ROI and leave the US with more resources or power. But as the US spends down its various resources and loses power, it has less and less options it can pursue to become stronger and hold on to power.

Additionally, almost all of the forces I describe in this blog post have cascading second-order and third-order effects which create a self-reinforcing decline in America's power. For example:

  • Decrease in soft power results in decreased ability to implement foreign policy that benefits the US and increases the chance that US dominance can be repeatedly undermined
  • Decrease in domestic trust in the US establishment results in lack of ability to implement domestic policy that benefits the US and do anything in general, which results in less soft power and decreases domestic trust
  • Weak finances lead to reduced soft power and increased reliance on money printing which creates inflation, reduces domestic trust, and misallocates capital which reduces the efficiency of the economy, which leads to even weaker finances, and so on

As you can see, once a decline has begun, it tends to continue (and perhaps even accelerate) through these deterministic cause-effect relationships. Without some opposing force to reverse its decline, America will continue to become weaker.

We are witnessing a fall in slow motion. Where will it stop?
