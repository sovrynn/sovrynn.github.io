Crypto Endgame - BTC vs ETH
By Junho (August 21, 2022) back

One reason why crypto is fascinating is because it’s so new. We get to apply old existing ideas in new situations and predict what might happen in the future. This is what I do in this post to try and speculate on what the future of BTC vs ETH looks like.

Crypto’s Endgame - The World’s Future Money

To recap, crypto’s utopian end goal is to create the world’s future money. Currently I expect this to be the most valuable use case of crypto. People commonly say that a crypto that becomes the world’s future money would take value from some combination of gold, fiat currency, and real estate/stocks.

An important detail is that money will be winner take all. I think this makes sense - there’s just more liquidity and less slippage, more precise informational signal, and no need to pay fees when converting between different forms of money. If multiple forms of money exist, they will always be competing, and the better form of money will eventually win out.

Is ETH Even Money?

BTC fits pretty cleanly into the money narrative (eventual fixed supply of 21M), but ETH is a bit of a frankenstein. It used to be a commodity that powers the ETH network. Now people are saying it’s an equity stake earning real yield (EIP-1559 + POS) from the ETH network, or an “internet bond” that other digital yields can be priced against. At first, I thought that ETH can’t be money because it doesn’t fit into the simple “fixed supply of tokens” model like Bitcoin and fiat do. If ETH becomes money, then money will be coupled to blockchain infrastructure, pay out issuance to people who secure the blockchain, and burn supply when people pay for digital transactions. However, I think it’s important to think outside the box. It seems strange now but I’m sure there are many many forms of technology today that would seem strange to earlier generations. And upon closer inspection, BTC as money is also coupled to blockchain infrastructure - miners get a block reward, and BTC might need tail emission for long-term security. “21M fixed token supply” is more a narrative than reality. The US dollar system is also pretty strange - it’s minted in cash and coins by the government but also exists digitally, is connected to the borrowing/lending of the US government and banks, and its supply can be manipulated by changing interest rates or reserve requirements or selling bonds to banks.

So, I don’t think ETH is disqualified as money based on its architecture alone.

Additionally, I think even if ETH doesn’t become money (in the way that we use money today), it’s possible that it could become even more valuable than money. But this is an idea to be explored in another post.

BTC vs ETH Comparison as Money

What does BTC have going for it?

Bitcoin has a better brand and network effect. Today, there are many more people that value Bitcoin than value Ether - I believe Bitcoiners are a superset of Ethereans. BTC also has a massive lead when it comes to institutional demand and nation-state adoption. Here’s some data to back up these claims:

  • Blackrock is launching a Bitcoin (not Ethereum!) trust
  • Grayscale's largest holding is Bitcoin
  • Bitcoin has a larger marketcap and more trading volume

BTC is also more compact than ETH. Its blockchain doesn’t have the bloat that ETH does. Today, Bitcoin’s blockchain is about 400 GB while an Ethereum archive node is about 10 TB. This is important when considering decentralization and long-term sustainability.

What does ETH have going for it?

ETH has better “pumpamentals” after EIP-1559 and the Merge - it will have less inflation as a % of supply after the Merge, as well as supply burning due to EIP-1559. The inflation will also be given to stakers, who have low costs, rather than miners, who have significant costs. These factors will help ETH’s price go up. ETH also has a “real-yield” due to EIP-1559 and POS staking. This, in my opinion, is by far the biggest advantage ETH will have over Bitcoin.

ETH also has the “X-factor” of decentralized programmability but I tend to be somewhat skeptical of this aspect of ETH. I think money competes more on network effect than being able to program it on the blockchain. I don’t see why useful applications cannot be built on top of Bitcoin even if they aren’t built into the base layer.

Who Cares if ETH Flippens Bitcoin if ETH is Not Money?

One hypothetical scenario is where ETH flippens BTC in the short-term, but it’s not sustainable. This is because, ultimately, cryptos need to have increasing demand to continue going up in price. And I don’t think bubbles qualify as sustainable demand. Personally I think crypto will only have sustainable demand by being the world’s digital money. So ETH could have a big run, but if people don’t eventually agree that it’s money, then it won’t have continued upside.

My current response to this hypothetical scenario is that “narrative follows price” - as ETH grows, a narrative will form around it. BTC and ETH are very much chameleons - even as the tech remains the same, people can spin up all kinds of ingenious narratives to market it to other people. As ETH flippens BTC, the narrative that ETH is a better money than BTC will follow.

I don’t think such a narrative can necessarily “market itself to fruition”. But it can help create continued demand in the short-term.

Gresham’s Law - Mechanics of Money Competition

One primitive which I think is relevant here is Gresham's Law. Essentially, it states that when people are choosing between different forms of money, they will choose to keep the money with more intrinsic value, and spend the money with less intrinsic value. However, “In the absence of effectively enforced legal tender laws, Gresham's law tends to operate in reverse; good money drives bad money out of circulation because people can decline to accept the less valuable money as a means of payment in transactions. But when all currency units are legally mandated to be recognized at the same face value, the traditional version of Gresham's law operates”.

Basically, if governments were to mandate that one must accept both ETH and BTC as money, then BTC would drive ETH out of circulation since ETH has some “real value” - it can be used as a commodity for the ETH network, has better pumpamentals, and earns yield. But if people can choose, then ETH would drive BTC out of circulation because people would refuse to get paid in BTC.

When considering possibilities through the lens of Gresham’s Law, the possibilities seem to favor ETH more since the second scenario seems a lot more realistic. Of course, this is all contingent on the fact that there are no technical problems with ETH.

Another comment I’d like to make is that we are nowhere near the point where people actually use crypto as money. Most people in crypto today are just speculators - people will only start to use crypto as money once lots of people value it.

How/When Will the Market “Choose” Between BTC and ETH?

I don’t think the market is a black box - people will choose between BTC and ETH for pragmatic and clear reasons. But obviously there’s a lot of uncertainty in the future and we don’t know exactly how things will play out.

There’s a famous term called hyperbitcoinization which describes a process where the majority of people in the world will flee to crypto. I think the monetization competition between BTC and ETH will likely not coincide perfectly with hypercryptoization, and will be a long, drawn-out process. Maybe the process could take decades.

Bitcoin’s Schelling Point of Being The First Crypto

Some Bitcoin maximalists argue that Ethereum, along with all other cryptos, are simply copies of Bitcoin. The idea is that these copies will ultimately fail because the market will decide that the first crypto is the only “legitimate” one. Here are some relevant links:

  • Bitcoin Can't Be Copied
  • @Croesus_BTC tweet thread

Ultimately I think Bitcoin’s Schelling Point is not a law of the universe but simply a strong advantage. Most cryptos that are trying to compete with Bitcoin will fail to overcome its numerous advantages, but it’s not impossible. There have been multiple forms of money in the past and what happened? They outcompeted each other. And it’s not the case that the earliest one always won. Otherwise we would have never progressed from the earliest forms of money to the money that we have today.

So, I think the competition is on.

Believers Drive Reality - The Theory of Reflexivity

George Soros coined the theory of reflexivity, which proposes that people’s beliefs can kick-start a self-reinforcing loop that makes people’s expectations shape what happens in the future.

Bitcoin and Ethereum both have very strong communities of people that believe in them, at least in the short-term. These people will directly help drive the next bull-run, and thanks to the cycle of reflexivity, help pump Bitcoin and Ethereum.

I do think that on a long-term perspective, Bitcoin’s community is stronger than Ethereum’s. Ethereum is newer and changing frequently, creating more uncertainty in its future, whereas Bitcoin’s holders tend to be more long-term oriented than Ethereum holders.

Regardless, Ethereum and Bitcoin will both be here for the next bull-run.

Closing Thoughts

One area for improvement in my analysis is that I feel I’m missing data. There’s historical events/data I can consider, as well as recent data that could help me compare Bitcoin and Ethereum in terms of adoption today. I feel I’m mostly dealing in ideas and narratives. It’s definitely an area I want to improve on.

Short-term I’m overweight Ether. Crypto is going to have more money coming into the space and I believe Ethereum will be one of the strong performers over the short-term due to Merge pumpamentals and ESG/internet-bond narrative, and being the natural “next choice” for institutions interested in branching out of Bitcoin for “diversification” or more upside.

Long-term I tend to be more of a believer in Bitcoin. I think Bitcoin is “good enough” and without additional information, everything else is just a trade. Ethereum is obviously the strongest contender but there’s just a lot of uncertainty in its future. But of course Bitcoin suffers from constant sell pressure from miners. Ultimately it’s more of a “pick your poison” than maximalism for me. I want to own both medium-term.

The BTC vs ETH debate is definitely an open question. I’m excited to see what the future brings for these two.
