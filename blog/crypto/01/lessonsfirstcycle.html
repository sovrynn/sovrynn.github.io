Lessons From My First Crypto Cycle
By Henry J (August 2, 2022) back

This post will cover some high-level lessons about investing in crypto which I took away from the most recent cycle. I have arrived at these points based on my own (limited) experiences as well as the wisdom of others. I recognize that there is almost certainly additional nuance to these lessons which I will only be able to understand by gaining more experience. And with those disclaimers out of the way, here are the lessons!

Be a Profit Maximalist, Not a Coin Maximalist

Be a profit maximalist, first and foremost, not a coin maximalist. More specifically, don’t be blindly married to one coin or narrative that is going to be the “one moonshot trade”. There are many, many ways to make money in this market, and your favorite coin is almost certainly not the most profitable trade that you can make. This market is already way too big for any single person to have “perfect information” on everything that’s going on.

Know What You Don’t Know - Don’t Blindly Assume Things About The Future

This lesson is about understanding what you don’t know, which includes many, many things in the future. As a crypto newbie I found myself having complete certainty that certain cryptos I were invested in would reach high price levels in the future. This certainty came from ignorance and was based on assumptions which weren’t necessarily true.

A good thought experiment for me was to consider how much certainty I had when it came to the future of other assets such as a company, real estate, or even a country’s currency. Generally speaking, I find that I have reasonable uncertainty regarding the future of these assets - a company may not be very profitable or even exist, a property’s location may cease to be popular, and countries can decay. This uncertainty is especially evident on longer timeframes, such as 20 or 50 years. Considering this, I think it doesn’t make sense that one would be able to predict, with high certainty, the future of any cryptocurrency. Cryptos are much more speculative, don’t have much grounding in the real world, and haven’t been around as long.

One actionable way to incorporate this lesson is to question all your assumptions - even the most basic assumptions. For example, is it guaranteed that Bitcoin will have another bull-run? Is it guaranteed that Ethereum’s DeFi and smart contract ecosystem will continue to grow for the next ten years? Questioning even basic assumptions like this is how you can refine your mental model of crypto.

What Drives Price? Only Two Things - Supply and Demand

It’s easy to parrot this fact but its all that matters. You can’t just focus on narratives if you want to figure out where the price is going to go, especially in the short-term. If you’re trying to understand and predict prices, you need to reason about things like:

  • Market structure - who are the participants in the market, and how do they relate to supply and demand
  • Changes to supply or demand

Of course, the best thing is real data. Ideally you’d like to get as close to the source as possible, by looking at data from exchanges or on-chain analytics/addresses. This is not something that I’ve done yet but I’m pretty sure that it is definitely very useful input.

One related topic which has also been important recently is the macro - what kinds of monetary actions or policies governments are taking in response to various global factors, and how this affects supply/demand/expectations in the crypto market. I’m certainly not going to pretend like this gives me an edge but at the very least you should understand the basics and know that this input exists. It’s certainly an area that I want to learn more on in the future.

Timing Exact Tops and Bottoms Is Impossible

Timing exact tops and bottoms is impossible because tops and bottoms are indeterministic. They rely on the individual decisions of market participants, as well as fundamental events in the world which may move the market, and those things are impossible to predict without perfect knowledge of the world.

This goes along with the idea that markets are probabilistic. At any point in time there are always a number of possibilities and we can try to assign certain probabilities to these possibilities.

As far as actionable advice goes for this lesson, I would say that as long as you have decided on a strategy with well-justified entry/exits, you should just be satisfied with your results without cherry-picking what could have been if you had timed the exact tops and bottoms. Because, as we’ve just covered, that’s impossible.

Missing Out on Making Money is Fine. Managing Risk And Having a Plan Is More Important

I think it’s important to be fine with not making the maximum amount of money, and to be fine with missing out on a good trade. Since we know timing exact tops and bottoms is impossible, it also follows that making the maximum amount of money should be impossible as well. But another way of looking at it is that if you attempt to make the maximum amount of money, you usually end up also taking on more risk.

In general, I think simply attempting to maximize return is not a proper framework for making investing decisions. This is because markets are indeterministic and always have multiple possibilities with different probabilities of occurring. Attempting to maximize return usually “picks” one of the possibilities but disregards the other ones. This may be fine if you have extremely high conviction but could backfire if things don’t work out how you expect. Instead, a better investing framework takes into account all the possibilities and seeks to maximize return while also taking into account the various possibilities and risks.

Avoid The Risk of Getting Blown Up at All Cost

You never want to get blown up (lose your entire portfolio), by definition. Because you need to keep playing the game to make money, and if you get blown up, it’s basically game over.

My mental model for reasoning about this is compounding risk. Essentially, when you have multiple risks that could each blow your entire portfolio up, you have to avoid all of them in order to stay alive. Some examples of compounding risks in crypto are holding your coins on exchanges/CeFi, not derisking after a pump, holding coins with lower structural demand, and using leverage. Once you start to compound risks like these, the odds of taking a big hit on your portfolio start to become quite significant.

As a thought experiment, let’s say you executed five subsequent trades, each with an extremely high probability of success (90%) but a small probability of getting blown up (10%). What happens if you decide to go all in on these trades to attempt to make the most money possible? The odds of any one of the trades succeeding is quite high, but the odds of succeeding in all the trades is only 59% (0.9^5) - not much better than a coin flip. If you lower the probability of success in each of the trades to 80%, you end up with only a 33% chance of not being blown up.

So what’s the lesson? Minimize risks that could blow up your entire bag, because risks compound.

Understand What Timeframe You Are Trading On, and What You Can and Cannot Do

I used to think you could draw a line between investing and trading but I think the difference is a bit more nuanced now. As far as crypto goes, investing and trading both involve understanding market participants and how they will affect supply and demand. But I do think broad categorizations can help crystallize understanding of what one is doing. So, what do I consider the difference between trading and investing?

Trading could be anything on shorter timeframes, perhaps anything from micro timeframes to several months. Investing would be longer timeframes like six months and up. Trading could be based on technical analysis or predicting volatility around certain events or short-term narratives. Investing would be based on larger trends around adoption or crypto cycles.

Based on those definitions, I think trading is hard and I don’t currently have an edge in it, on any timeframe. But I do think I can reason about longer term trends, which I consider investing.

And that’s really what this lesson is all about - figuring out the meta-strategy of your investing strategy.

Don’t Blindly Follow People’s Advice

There were many times in the past cycle when I blindly followed people’s advice regarding a certain coin, or future price action. I don’t think this is a good idea anymore. In general, I think whenever you are considering blindly following someone’s advice, it’s a signal that you have low conviction and need to do more research or get more experience.

One consequence of blindly following advice is that you can easily get shaken out of your position, because you have low conviction and don’t have a rational thesis behind your actions. Another consequence of blindly following advice is that you usually can’t get real-time updates when the original person changes their mind. This can be pretty important in a volatile and quickly-changing market like crypto.

Of course, there is a difference between blindly following people’s advice, versus getting useful information and different perspectives from others. The latter is important and something you should always be doing.

I think the only situation where it makes sense to blindly follow someone’s advice is if you trust them and are also in some kind of mentorship relationship with them where you are effectively trusting them to continuously manage your money.

Fin

And that’s all my lessons from my first cycle! Obviously, investing (managing finances?) is a lifelong journey. And I may be able to glean new lessons from these same experiences in the future. But, I consider this a pretty useful checkpoint for myself.
