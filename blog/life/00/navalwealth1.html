1/X Naval on Wealth (The Metagame of Wealth)
By Henry J (November 15, 2022) back

This series of posts covers a podcast by Naval Ravikant, a tech entrepreneur, about how to get wealthy. It’s an amazing podcast where Naval basically gives us an “idea hose” of incredible, incredible ideas. In my posts, I try to organize the ideas, into topics and within topics, and summarize them. Sometimes it’s hard to distill down what Naval says without losing some of the nuance because the information is already so dense. But, I do my best.

This first post is about understanding the metagame of wealth. Naval helps us understand what the game of wealth is and what its characteristics are. Naval also compares it to the game of status, which gives us an increasing understanding of the nature of the game of wealth.

1. What are wealth, money, and status, and how should we think about them?

What is Wealth?

Wealth is investments that earn you passive income while you sleep. Businesses or real estate or some kind of intellectual property.

Wealth buys you freedom so you don’t have to be a slave, on a collar, moving around life at the whim of someone else. Wealth gives you the power to not have to wear a tie, wake up for a meeting you don’t really care about, or spend hours commuting in traffic every day. Wealth allows you to be your own sovereign individual - you can wake up when you want, do what you want, you don’t have to do anything you don’t want to do or be anywhere you don’t want to be.

In a nutshell, the purpose of wealth is freedom. Nothing more. Specifically, wealth is NOT about buying designer clothing or sailing around in yachts or driving Ferarris. Naval says this stuff gets really boring and really stupid really fast.

You’re not going to get wealth unless you really want it. It’s competitive - the entire world wants it, and is working hard at it. There’s a finite amount of resources on the planet, and to get the resources to do what you want and become wealthy, you have to stand out.

That being said, wealth is a positive-sum game, and Naval thinks that fundamentally, everyone can be wealthy. Everyone can create something valuable for society.

What is Money?

From my time in crypto I’ve come to understand it as an informational system that signals how much of society’s resources/labor/services a person “owns” based on what they’ve done for society. Naval describes it similarly but much more eloquently, saying that money is trying to be a reliable IOU from society that you are owed something for something that you, or someone who gave you the money, did in the past. It’s a credit for a piece of what society produces that can be transferred across time and space (borrowing from the great Michael Saylor), and you can get it by doing something useful for society.

What is Status? Zero-sum game of status vs positive-sum game of wealth

If there is a game of wealth, there is also a game of status. Status is a very hierarchical game - who’s #1, who’s #2, who’s #3. It’s a very old game that we’ve been playing since we’ve lived in primitive “monkey-tribes” long ago. It’s a game that decides who is where in the social hierarchy.

Fundamentally, status games are zero-sum. For someone to win, another person always has to lose. For person #3 to move up to position #2, someone else has to move down to position #3.

Examples of status games today are politics and sports.

Naval doesn’t love status games. He thinks they play an important role in society to help figure out who’s in charge, but we only play them because they’re a necessary evil. And they were much more important in the past. Historically, status was a better indicator of survival than wealth. You couldn’t have wealth before farming-based societies. Hunter-gatherers lived entirely in status-based societies. But nowadays, society is more wealth-based.

Yet, even though society today is more wealth-based, there is always a subtle competition going on between the money game and the status game. For example, when journalists attack rich people, or the tech industry, for trying to make money. They are really trying to up their own status by putting themselves on a moral high ground.

Fundamentally, Naval says you should try to avoid playing status games. It makes you an angry person because you’re always fighting to put other people down, to put yourself up. It’s a zero-sum game, as opposed to a positive-sum game.

2. Is Making Money Evil? Did Billionaires Steal Their Wealth From Others?

This trope that wealthy people stole their wealth from others is propaganda, and incorrectly conflates different things.

A better way to reason about it is to consider the predator-prey relationship between makers and takers which exists throughout nature. There are people who go out and create things, and then there are people who come along with a sword or a gun or taxes or communism or crony capitalism and steal from the makers. We can draw a metaphor to parasites in nature here.

Fundamentally, we have to understand that wealth is positive-sum. Obviously we can create jobs and technology and “valuable stuff”, otherwise we would still be living like cavemen making fires out of sticks. In fact, almost anyone living today is far wealthier than anyone living 300 years ago. Back then, nobody had antibiotics, cars, electricity, iPhones, etc. All of these inventions have made humans wealthier as a species. A poor person in a poor country today is living far better than the richest person in 1400s Europe.

A thought experiment that Naval proposes is what would happen if everyone had the skills to build stuff (software and hardware engineering skills). Then in 20 years, society would build robots to do everything, and would have robot-driven hospitals, self-driving cars, clean energy, etc. Then, everyone would be retired, and if people still worked, they would be doing it out of free will, to express themselves and contribute and build things.

Naval ascribes the word “capitalism” to this concept of humans interacting, in a free market, motivated by free will, to transact, contract, and participate in the positive-sum game of wealth. Capitalism is innate to humans. It just gets hijacked by bad actors and things like corruption, monopolies, and crony capitalism.

So the idea that wealth is stolen and capitalism is bad is really just propaganda characterizing wealth as a zero-sum game and this (false) idea is proliferated by the people who are playing the status game.

Just to shill the game of wealth one more time to you, the reader - the future will bring more and more wealth and abundance for everybody. Everybody can be wealthy, retired, and successful - it’s merely a question of education and desire. You have to want it. If you don’t want it, that’s fine - you just opt out of the game. But don’t put down the people that are playing the game. This is the game that gives you a bed to sleep in and a roof over your head at night, the supermarkets stocked with food, and the iPhone buzzing in your pocket. It’s a beautiful game that is worth playing ethically, rationally, morally, and socially. It’s going to continue to make us all richer and richer.

3. Thoughts on countries/organizations/groups/political parties that openly denigrate wealth creation

These organizations are playing the zero-sum game of status. In the process, these people destroy the process of wealth creation and drag down the entire group to their level.

Groups that despise wealth creation will fail. It’s what happens when you have too many takers and not enough makers. It’s what’s happening in Venezuela. It’s what happens in communism/socialism. Too much reallocation and division and not enough production. Like an organism having too many parasites.

If a society does not respect ethical wealth creation based on free markets, free will, and innate human nature, that society will plunge into ruin and darkness.
