You Are the Placebo
By Henry J (April 4, 2023) back

This blog post is my synopsis of "You Are the Placebo" by Dr. Joe Dispenza. It's a fascinating book. Honestly, I think it might change my life.

The key idea is that our thoughts may be much, much, much more powerful than we think. This specific book focuses on the power of our thoughts to create physiological changes in our body. However, this book also aligns with books like "Atomic Habits" and (I think) "The Secret".

Also, it's a pretty rough summary. Better to get it done than to have a perfect-or-nothing approach.

Preface

In this section, the author gives his backstory. Apparently, he was in a terrible, terrible car accident which completely destroyed his back. Going against what all the doctors told him, he decided not to get surgery. Instead, he visualized and imagined his good health, which manifested in his body actually healing itself, and now he has no health problems.

The author also talks about how people who attend his seminars or follow his teachings created similar physiological changes in themselves which, following conventional medical wisdom, would not be possible.

Ch 1: A compilation of anecdotes

This chapter covers many stories of how people's thoughts created significant physiological changes.

For example, one man who was told that he had cancer, literally thought himself to death. His autopsy revealed that he didn't die from cancer at all and should have been healthy.

There are many stories where people take placebos (in scientific studies for example) and create significant physiological changes even though they only took a placebo.

Assuming all the stories are true (which I believe they are), this chapter makes us question the power of thought.

Ch 2: Could the placebo effect be real?

Based on these anecdotes, what we think can create profound positive, but also negative changes in our body. Are thoughts self-fulfilling prophecies?

Joe mentions one specific study where a placebo group ended up creating more endorphins in themselves. If this is the case, then by extension, shouldn't thoughts be able to influence our bodies to create various other kinds of compounds which could have a multitude of different possible effects on our bodies?

Another study Joe mentions is the classic conditioning experiment done by Pavlov, who trained dogs to salivate when hearing a bell by associating the bell with food. But we should think more closely about this because the implications are profound. The end result is a bell, which has no connection to food, causes the dogs to experience a whole host of physiological changes such as salivating and producing stomach acid possibly. It's not the bell that's producing those effects. It's the thoughts that are going on in the dogs' heads.

Joe mentions another study where rats were fed sweetened water mixed with an immuno-supressing drug. The interesting thing was that even after later removing the drug, the rats that were fed sweetened water still had their immune systems supressed, supposedly because of their conditioning.

If this is all true, then what kinds of second-order effects might we be embedding in our bodies, which don't have to be there?

Next, Joe talks about how placebos work even if the subjects know it's a placebo.

Ch 3: The placebo effect in the brain

This chapter is about some knitty-gritty processes concerning the brain.

Joe talks about how the same thoughts will lead to the same choices which will lead to the same actions/behaviors which will lead to the same experiences which will then conjure the same thoughts.

In order to change this cycle, we must become aware of it. Become aware of what choices we make and what kinds of thoughts and emotions we have. And if we want to change it, we must take new actions, which create new experiences, which will reinforce our new self.

One idea which is commonly talked about but good to rehash is the structure of our brain (an incredibly dense network of neurons). When neurons fire, it actually changes the physical structure, enforcing the connection that just fired and wiring it into physical reality so it lasts. Not actionable advice necessarily but good to go through this mental model again.

Joe gives a thought experiment of someone who has a bad public speaking experience and then relives it the days after, and retells it to his friends. In the future, any public speaking opportunity will remind this person of that one failure and he will become scared. If you think back to the Pavlov conditioning study, this person is essentially conditioning their bad public speaking skills. And embedding it into their brain, automatic reactions, and physiology. They have let their one bad experience become who they are when it comes to public speaking.

Ch 4: The placebo effect in the body

Joe talks about epigenetics and how our genetic code is a blueprint, but our body determines what parts of the genetic code to "activate". So genetic determinism isn't accurate. Your genes have many many possibilities and you can activate different ones.

Stress affects your epigenetics, and so do positive emotions.

Ch 5: Putting it all together

Clearly our thoughts, emotions, and experiences influence our epigenetics and physiological state. So the question becomes, can we frontrun this process by simply changing our perceptions, beliefs, and thoughts to influence ourselves ahead of an actual environmental change? The answer must be yes.

Joe names the technique that we can use to do this as "mental rehearsal", which is basically extremely explicit and vivid visualization.

Joe stresses that we also need a strong emotional input in order to optimally create this change.

Joe says we should avoid negative emotions, like fear and anger, and try to cultivate positive emotions such as gratitude, joy, and excitement, to maximize our health.

Ch 6: Suggestibility

Suggestibility is essentially how malleable you are to this process of changing yourself through thoughts and emotions.

Joe also talks about the analytical mind, which is the part of your mind which you are conscious about, and handles logic, wisdom, and what you've learned from past experiences.

Being analytical and being suggestible (at any given moment) are at odds with one another. Because being analytical, at its extreme, is all about being selfish and staying within the realm of possibilities you can reason about. Whereas suggestibility is about being open to new thoughts, experiences, and possibilities.

Obviously analytical people are open to new ideas. I'm not sure how useful this chapter is.

Ch 7: Attitudes, beliefs, and perceptions

This is an amazing chapter.

Essentially, experiences and memories create thoughts and feelings.

The repeated summation of thoughts and feelings is our attitudes. For example, if you have a series of good thoughts and feelings, you might say you have a good attitude. Vice versa for a bad attitude.

If you repeat or maintain certain attitudes long enough and string them together, that's how you create a belief. A belief is simply an extended state of being - thoughts and feelings that you keep thinking and feeling until you hardwire them into your brain and body.

If you string a group of related beliefs together, they form your perception of reality. That's a sustained state of being based on your beliefs, attitudes, thoughts, and feelings.

Finally, our perception of reality causes us to take certain choices and behaviors, and handle our relationships in certain ways. It all leaks out. Who we are leaks out into the world.

Joe gives a laundry list of negative perceptions which I never was aware I even had (but I could come up with if asked). I realized how limiting these beliefs are and want to change myself.

In order to change a belief, Joe says you have to start by first accepting that it's possible, then change your level of energy with the heightened emotion you read about earlier, and finally allow your biology to reorganize itself.

You also can't change your beliefs and perceptions just once. You have to reinforce that over and over. A great way to do this would be to wake up and visualize who you want to be. What kind of beliefs and perceptions you want to have. And that way those thoughts will linger and influence you throughout the rest of the day. Have to be aware.

Joe also says that experiences biologically change you - they neurologically wire and enrich the brain, and emotionally and chemically change the body.

Your actions become your experiences. If you want to change who you are, you change your thoughts, which changes your actions, which changes your experiences, which feeds back into you. And creates change.

Ch 8: The quantum mind

I wasn't expecting much from this chapter but it turned out to be a really nice one.

Joe makes a really really nice metaphor. At a high level, the world seems to operate deterministically. But at a low quantum level, matter doesn't exist until we attempt to observe it. And when we stop observing it, it vanishes. Yet the quantum clearly makes up the high-level world.

In the same way, our lives could be the quantum field - endless possibilities. But by fixating our mind on some outcome that we want, we can collapse, or influence, reality and the future into the possibility that we want.

As someone who believes in the fractal nature of the world, this metaphor speaks to me.

Joe says we should observe ourselves in a particular new future that is different from our past, then expect that reality to occur, and finally emotionally embrace the outcome. Then, at least just for a moment, we'll be living in that future reality, and conditioning our bodies to believe it existed in that future. So we can observe our future into reality.

On the flip side, if we, as the quantum observer of our lives, look at life from the same state of mind every day, then we will collapse the infinite possibilities of our lives into the same result day in and day out.

Imagining is manifesting.

The second half of the chapter is another great metaphor. Essentially, in quantum physics, the less energy an atom has, the easier it is to see in the physical world. Conversely, the more energy it has, the more time the atom will spend in the quantum field,where we cannot observe it. It's the same where if a fan is spinning slowly, you can see the blades, but the faster it spins, the harder it becomes to discern the blades.

Joe says the same thing happens for the energy in our mind. Creative and positive emotions make us have less matter and more energy, whereas survival emotions (which are negative) make us have less energy and more matter. This energy radiates out into the world, but also within ourselves. When we have high energy, our cells can communicate well and act in unison, allowing our body to work well. Conversely, when we have low energy, our cells don't communicate well and fall into disarray. It's a sliding scale from high order and low entropy (high energy, positive emotions) to low order and high entropy (low energy, stress, and eventually death).

By having the right thoughts, we can create the right energy (positive emotions) which will enhance our well-being and radiate positive energy into the world.

Ch 11: Meditation Preparation

Best time to meditate is before sleeping or after waking up.

The most interesting thing from this chapter is that during meditation, we try to free our minds from our normal thought patterns. Since we know thought patterns, developed through our past, control every aspect of our physical being in the future, staying in these thought patterns predetermines our future. By freeing ourselves from our normal thought patterns. We can open ourselves up to the different quantum possibilities of the future and not be limited by the past.
