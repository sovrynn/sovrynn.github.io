2/X Naval on Wealth (Meta-Strategy For Creating Wealth)
By Henry J (November 16, 2022) back

This second post contains some meta-strategies for creating wealth. In some sense that could describe the entire podcast but some of my other blog posts have a more granular title; this one is just filled with points that are mostly independent of each other.

4. Is creating wealth about getting lucky? What are the different kinds of luck?

Making money is not about luck; it’s about becoming the kind of person that makes money. Naval likes to think that if he lost all his money and was dropped in any English speaking country, within 5-10 years, he would become wealthy again. It’s about developing the right skillset. In 1000 parallel universes, you want to be deterministically wealthy in 999 of them, rather than wealthy by luck in 50 of them. You want to be able to factor luck out of it.

There’s 4 kinds of luck:

  • Blind luck. Something completely out of your control
  • Luck created through hard work, persistence, hustle, motion. When you’re running around taking many actions and doing many things, you’re stirring things up and creating lots of opportunities. You’re generating enough hustle and energy that luck will find you. It’s like having a high APM in a video game
  • Become good at spotting luck. If you’re in a specific field and you have more experience and knowledge than others, you might be able to spot the opportunities before others
  • Build a unique character/brand/mindset such that luck finds you. For example, let’s say you become known as the best underwater deep sea-diver. And one day, someone, by sheer luck, happens to find some treasure under the sea. Well, they will deterministically come to you to help get the treasure, and give you a cut. So their sheer luck became your deterministic luck

Naval says that he hasn’t made his money in one giant payout. Rather, he stacks up chips over time by consistently creating wealth over time - by creating businesses and opportunities and investments. You have to slowly build up the snowball over time.

Additionally, thanks to the Internet, opportunities to make wealth are more abundant than ever before. Naval says he has too many opportunities to create wealth, products, businesses and opportunities and as a byproduct get paid by society, that time is his limiting factor.

5. Expanding on the fourth type of luck - the most deterministic one

Out of the four kinds of luck described in the previous section, the most deterministic one is the fourth one. It’s so deterministic that it stops being luck, and its definition goes from luck to destiny.

One specific quality you want to cultivate is having the kind of reputation that makes people do deals through you. You want to become a trusted, reliable, high-integrity, long-term thinking dealmaker. Then, when other people want to do deals, but they don’t know how to do them in a trustworthy manner with strangers, they will come to you and do deals through you. In the process they might give you a cut of the deal, or a unique deal altogether. People might look at this and call it luck but you will know its not. An example of someone with this kind of reputation is Warren Buffet. Because of his reputation, he gets offered deals to buy companies and bail out banks that other people can’t do.

One caveat to reputation is that despite being very powerful, reputation is also fragile. It has accountability on the line - trust.

In order to create the fourth kind of luck, you will need to be very passionate about something. You can’t be normal or average. This is because the world is a very efficient place, so everyone has already dug through all the obvious places to dig. And so to find something new and novel and uncovered, you first are usually operating on a frontier, which requires passion. But more importantly you have to be willing to dig deeper than other people will dig or even consider rational, just because you’re interested.

By repeatedly pursuing all kinds of luck except dumb luck, you can increase your chances of success, and neutralize variance so that your own talents come into play. You can model this with a basic binomial distribution.

6. You won’t get rich renting out your time. Here’s why

Naval says this is one of the absolute most important points. People seem to think that you can create wealth and make money through work. And it’s probably not going to work.

Here’s a summary of the reasons why:

  • Your inputs are closely tied to your outputs. If you’re getting paid hourly, that means you have to put time in to get paid. So you’re not earning money when you sleep, not earning money when you’re retired, and not earning money when you’re on vacation. You also can’t earn non-linearly
  • You’re very replaceable, because you’re not taking on the risk and the accountability and the intellectual property and the brand. So you’re just going to get paid the bare minimum to do the job
  • You’re actually just not even creating anything much original or new for society. You’re just doing a set role - doing things over and over. This also makes you replaceable, because most of the time, such roles can be taught. So eventually, you’re going to be competing with a younger person who has more recent knowledge they were taught and who is coming in to replace you. You could also replaced by a robot or AI
  • Often times you’re not being creative

Everybody who really makes money at some point owns a piece of a product or a business or some kind of IP. Usually the real wealth is created by starting your own companies or investing in equity.

Stepping back from making companies, you just want a job or career or profession where your inputs don’t match your outputs. Businesses that have high creativity and high leverage are places where you could do an hour of work and have a huge effect or do 1000 hours of work and have no effect. Compare Satoshi (created Bitcoin) compared to someone working on the wrong software at some company where they work a lot but it goes nowhere. On the flip-side, if you compare lumberjacks, the best lumberjack in the world might be just 3 times better than the worst lumberjack in the world.

Stepping back from specific professions, at a more fundamental level, you want to look for things that are leveraged. And by leverage, we’re not just referring to financial leverage used by speculators. Tools are leverage - software or tractors or saws - and leverage is what helps you create outputs that are highly disconnected from your inputs. Tasks that require high creativity can also have this disconnect.

In summary, if you do something where your inputs and outputs are highly connected, it will be very very very hard to create wealth and make wealth for yourself in that process.

7. Don’t upgrade your lifestyle too fast if you want to become wealthy

People who are living far beneath their means enjoy a freedom that people busy upgrading their lifestyle just can’t fathom. Once you make a little bit of money, you still want to be living like your old self, just so the worry goes away.

What often happens is that people gradually upgrade their lifestyle as their wages gradually increase. And this upgrading of the lifestyle also ups what you consider as “wealth”, keeping you in this wage-slave cycle of making more -> having more -> wanting more -> having to make more.

8. You get money by creating the consumer products of the FUTURE that don’t exist today

Money is IOUs from society saying you did something good in the past, and now here’s something we owe you for the future. So, society will pay you for creating things that it wants. But, society doesn’t yet know how to create those things, because if it did, it would already be mass-producing those things, and consequently, it wouldn't need to pay you to produce them.

Applying this analogy to the past, everything that we use and that seems ubiquitous today, like everything on the street, or in your workplace or house, used to be technology at one time. Street lights, light bulbs, plumbing, printers, cars, iPhones, etc. But then they became adopted and mass produced and became products, and made the creators of them rich. Oil made Rockefeller rich. Cars made Henry Ford rich. Technology is just the set of things that don’t quite work yet. They haven’t become products yet.

So, society always wants new things. If you want to be wealthy, you need to figure out one of those things you can provide for society, that it doesn’t yet know how to get, but will want, and that you have the skillset to create. And then you have to create it. And then you have to scale it, so that everyone can have it.

9. The Internet has massively broadened career possibilities to include many extremely niche roles. Be your unique self. Escape competition through authenticity

The Internet connects everyone to each other on the planet. You can reach anyone through email, Twitter, social media, web browsing, etc. Anyone can reach anyone. This means that you can find the audience for your product, talent, or skill, no matter how far away they are geographically.

If we do a thought experiment of someone who makes niche videos. Without the Internet, how would they find their audience? They might have to go around in the neighborhood they live in and show people their videos on some display, or play it at their local movie theater. It would be impossible to find that niche audience. But with the Internet, this person can find the audience for these niche videos. Today, there are people who have found audiences for many niche obsessions - collecting snakes, riding hot air balloons, solo sailing, doing miniature Japanese cooking, etc. It might not be a massive audience, it could be really small (50k people), but if you want to find them, the Internet enables you to do that.

Because the Internet allows people to find niche audiences, people can find “buyers” for their uniqueness rather than conforming to a few roles in society. Everyone is completely different. Naval says it’s not even close. Everyone has different genetics, experiences, skill sets, and interests. So everyone can be creatively superb at their own unique thing. Some examples of very niche jobs today are esports players, bloggers, vloggers, Youtubers, podcasters, etc. But if we compare that to long ago, like if you lived in some fishing village in Italy, no one cared about your unique skill, so you had to conform to the few jobs that your village needed.

Naval says everyone is the best at being themselves, and that you can “escape competition through authenticity”. When you’re competing with people, it’s because you’re copying them. But instead of imitating, if you do your own thing and be yourself, you can escape competition. Because no one can compete with you on being you.

Note: It goes without saying but I want to say that we shouldn’t take Naval’s words literally here. Obviously there isn’t an audience for everyone. But the Internet enables you to find a niche audience if they exist.
